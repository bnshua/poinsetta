Treatment

What to do if your animal eats part of the plant

Most poinsettia reactions are mild, but you still want to handle it fast. Your pet doesn’t deserve a holiday stomach apocalypse.

1. Remove Plant Material

Stop the snack before it keeps going.

  • Gently take away the plant or any chewed leaves.
  • Check for pieces stuck in the mouth or teeth.
  • If safe, wipe sap off lips with a damp cloth.

2. Rinse + Offer Water

Sap can irritate the mouth, so flush it out.

  • Offer fresh water to drink.
  • If your pet lets you, wipe/rinse the mouth gently.
  • Don’t force water down their throat.
Make sure to lightly give them water. Be careful with them.

3. Monitor for 24 Hours

Most symptoms show up early and fade quickly.

  • Watch for drooling, vomiting, diarrhea, or lethargy.
  • Keep them indoors so you can track behavior.
  • Make sure they still drink and pee normally.

4. Feed Lightly (If Needed)

Only if their stomach seems upset.

  • Wait a bit if they vomited before giving food.
  • Offer a small bland meal (plain chicken/rice).
  • Don’t give treats or rich food right away.

If they refuse food totally, that’s a sign to call.

5. When to Call the Vet

If anything looks worse instead of better.

  • Vomiting/diarrhea lasts longer than ~24 hours
  • Repeated vomiting (more than 2–3 times)
  • Swelling around mouth or face
  • Breathing trouble
  • Extreme weakness or collapse
  • Your pet is very young, old, or already ill
Trust your gut. If it feels wrong, call.

6. Emergency Tip

Useful if you need to go in.

  • Bring a photo of the plant or a piece of it.
  • Tell the vet about how much was eaten and when.
  • Note any symptoms and their timing.
